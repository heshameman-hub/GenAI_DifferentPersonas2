## zero_shot

**Prompt:**
Explain how rainbows are formed.

**Output:**
The formation of a rainbow is a beautiful demonstration of the physics of light, requiring three main processes: **refraction**, **dispersion**, and **reflection** within individual raindrops.

Here is a step-by-step explanation of how these processes combine to create the familiar arc of colored light.

---

## 1. The Necessary Ingredients

For a rainbow to form, two conditions must be met:

1.  **Sunlight:** The sun must be shining brightly and generally relatively low in the sky (the lower the sun, the higher the visible arc of the rainbow).
2.  **Water Droplets:** There must be a source of suspended water droplets (rain, mist, or spray) in the air **opposite** the sun.

You must always have your back to the sun to see a rainbow.

## 2. The Three Physics Processes

A rainbow forms because each raindrop acts as a tiny prism, separating the sunlight into its constituent colors.

### A. Step 1: Refraction (Bending the Light)

As a ray of white sunlight enters a raindrop, it moves from one medium (air) to another (water). This change in medium causes the light ray to **bend** (refract).

*   The light enters the front surface of the spherical raindrop and bends inward.

### B. Step 2: Dispersion (Separating the Colors)

This is the crucial step where the colors become visible. White sunlight is actually a spectrum of colors (red, orange, yellow, green, blue, indigo, violet—ROYGBIV), and each color has a slightly different wavelength.

*   When light refracts, the amount it bends depends on its wavelength. **Shorter wavelengths (like violet and blue)** bend more severely than **longer wavelengths (like red).**
*   Because the colors bend at slightly different angles inside the raindrop, the white light is split into its separate component colors. This separation is called **dispersion.**

### C. Step 3: Internal Reflection

After the light has been dispersed inside the raindrop, it travels to the back surface of the drop.

*   Instead of passing straight out, the light ray hits the back surface at a specific angle and is **reflected** back toward the front of the drop (where it first entered).

### D. Step 4: Second Refraction and Emission

The separated and reflected rays of light travel back to the front surface of the drop.

*   As the light rays leave the water and re-enter the air, they refract (bend) a second time, magnifying the separation of colors.
*   This dispersed light is then directed back toward the observer.

## 3. Why the Arc Shape and Color Order?

The rainbow is not a flat image; it is perceived as an arc due to the specific geometry of the light returning to the observer.

### The Angle of Return

The processes of refraction, dispersion, and reflection cause the maximum intensity of the dispersed light to be focused at specific angles relative to the observer's shadow (the anti-solar point).

*   **Red light** emerges at an angle of about **42 degrees** from the anti-solar point.
*   **Violet light** emerges at an angle of about **40 degrees** from the anti-solar point.

Because all the raindrops that are reflecting red light at 42 degrees form a perfect circle around the anti-solar point, and all the drops reflecting violet light at 40 degrees form another perfect circle, the observer sees the rainbow as an arc.

### The Color Order

In a primary rainbow (the most common type):

*   **Red** is always on the **outside** (top) because it bends the least and emerges at the highest angle (42°).
*   **Violet** is always on the **inside** (bottom) because it bends the most and emerges at the lowest angle (40°).

## Summary

In essence, a rainbow is an optical illusion where millions of raindrops simultaneously capture, separate, and redirect sunlight back to your eye. Every drop you look at is sending you a different color, and the combined effect of all the drops at those precise angles creates the brilliant, colored arc we call a rainbow.

## chain_of_thought

**Prompt:**
Solve step by step:
If a train travels 60 km/h for 2 hours, then 90 km/h for 1 hour, what is the average speed?

**Output:**
This problem requires calculating the average speed, which is defined as the total distance traveled divided by the total time taken.

$$\text{Average Speed} = \frac{\text{Total Distance}}{\text{Total Time}}$$

---

## Step 1: Calculate the distance traveled in the first segment

The train travels at $60 \text{ km/h}$ for $2 \text{ hours}$.

$$\text{Distance}_1 = \text{Speed} \times \text{Time}$$
$$\text{Distance}_1 = 60 \text{ km/h} \times 2 \text{ h} = 120 \text{ km}$$

## Step 2: Calculate the distance traveled in the second segment

The train travels at $90 \text{ km/h}$ for $1 \text{ hour}$.

$$\text{Distance}_2 = \text{Speed} \times \text{Time}$$
$$\text{Distance}_2 = 90 \text{ km/h} \times 1 \text{ h} = 90 \text{ km}$$

## Step 3: Calculate the total distance traveled

Add the distances from both segments.

$$\text{Total Distance} = \text{Distance}_1 + \text{Distance}_2$$
$$\text{Total Distance} = 120 \text{ km} + 90 \text{ km} = 210 \text{ km}$$

## Step 4: Calculate the total time taken

Add the time from both segments.

$$\text{Total Time} = 2 \text{ hours} + 1 \text{ hour} = 3 \text{ hours}$$

## Step 5: Calculate the average speed

Divide the total distance by the total time.

$$\text{Average Speed} = \frac{\text{Total Distance}}{\text{Total Time}}$$
$$\text{Average Speed} = \frac{210 \text{ km}}{3 \text{ hours}} = 70 \text{ km/h}$$

---

**Answer:** The average speed of the train is **70 km/h**.

## step_by_step

**Prompt:**
Let's reason step by step:
What happens to water when it’s heated to 100°C at sea level?

**Output:**
Here is a step-by-step reasoning of what happens to water when it is heated to $100^\circ\text{C}$ at sea level:

### Step 1: Define the Initial Conditions
*   **Substance:** Water ($\text{H}_2\text{O}$).
*   **Initial State:** Likely liquid (unless specified otherwise, heating implies starting below the boiling point).
*   **Temperature Reached:** $100^\circ\text{C}$.
*   **Pressure:** Standard atmospheric pressure at sea level (approximately 1 atmosphere or 101.325 kPa).

### Step 2: Define Key Physical Constants (Boiling Point)
*   The **boiling point** is the temperature at which a substance changes from a liquid to a gas (vapor) throughout the bulk of the liquid.
*   For pure water, the boiling point at standard atmospheric pressure (sea level) is **$100^\circ\text{C}$ ($212^\circ\text{F}$)**.

### Step 3: Analyze the Process Leading Up to $100^\circ\text{C}$
*   As the water is heated from room temperature (e.g., $20^\circ\text{C}$) up toward $100^\circ\text{C}$, the liquid water absorbs thermal energy.
*   This absorbed energy increases the kinetic energy of the water molecules, causing the temperature of the liquid to rise.

### Step 4: Analyze the Event at $100^\circ\text{C}$
*   When the water reaches $100^\circ\text{C}$ at sea level pressure, the vapor pressure of the liquid water becomes equal to the external atmospheric pressure.
*   This condition initiates **boiling**.

### Step 5: Describe the Observable Phenomenon (Boiling)
*   The water undergoes a phase transition (vaporization).
*   Large bubbles of water vapor form throughout the volume of the liquid, rise quickly to the surface, and burst, releasing steam (gaseous water) into the atmosphere.

### Step 6: Determine the Final State
*   If heating continues past the point where $100^\circ\text{C}$ is reached, the temperature of the water **will not rise** above $100^\circ\text{C}$ until **all** the liquid water has converted into steam.
*   All the added heat energy (the latent heat of vaporization) is used solely to break the intermolecular bonds and complete the phase change.

---

### Conclusion

When water is heated to $100^\circ\text{C}$ at sea level pressure, the water **begins to boil** (or is actively boiling). It undergoes a phase change from liquid water to gaseous water (steam).

## The Cookie Sorting Puppy Analogy

Imagine you have a big pile of cookies, and they come in two main types:

1. **Chocolate Chip Cookies (Type C):** Round, slightly rough.
2. **Oatmeal Raisin Cookies (Type O):** Bumpy, slightly flatter.
